$$\frac{dE}{d\sigma} = 2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(\sigma,t) \cdot \frac{\partial g}{\partial \sigma}(\sigma,t), dt$$

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \sigma}\log|\zeta(\sigma+it)| = \text{Re}!\left(\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}(\sigma+it)\right)$$

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \sigma}\log|\zeta(1-\sigma+it)| = -\text{Re}!\left(\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}(1-\sigma+it)\right)$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{\partial g}{\partial \sigma} = \text{Re}!\left(\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}(\sigma+it)\right) + \text{Re}!\left(\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}(1-\sigma+it)\right) = \text{Re}(F(\sigma+it))$$

$$\boxed{\frac{dE}{d\sigma} = 2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(\sigma,t) \cdot \text{Re}(F(\sigma+it)), dt}$$

$$g(\sigma, t) \cdot \text{Re}(F(\sigma+it)) = 0 \quad \text{pointwise}$$

This holds either when g = 0 (i.e., σ = ½) or when Re(F) = 0. Since Re(F(s)) = 0 on the zero set of ζ (where the logarithmic derivative has poles), this recovers ζ(ρ) = 0 as the equation of motion — exactly as claimed. The variational and endomorphic routes are consistent.

The cross-term for each pair is simply $\frac{1}{2}\frac{d}{d\sigma}|h_\rho|^2_{L^2}$. Asking whether the cross-term is positive is therefore equivalent to asking whether $|h_\rho|^2_{L^2}$ is increasing in σ away from ½.

AM-GM applied termwise: For a single paired zero (ρ, 1−ρ̄) with $\sigma_\rho = \frac{1}{2}$ (on the critical line):

$$h_\rho(\sigma, t) = \log\frac{|\sigma+it-\rho|}{|1-\sigma+it-\rho|}$$

At σ = ½, numerator and denominator are equal by mirror symmetry — $h_\rho = 0$, so $|h_\rho|^2_{L^2} = 0$.

For σ ≠ ½, the AM-GM inequality on the two distances gives:

$$\frac{|\sigma+it-\rho| + |1-\sigma+it-\rho|}{2} \geq \sqrt{|\sigma+it-\rho| \cdot |1-\sigma+it-\rho|}$$

with equality only when σ = ½. This forces $|h_\rho| > 0$, the $L^2$ norm grows strictly with |σ − ½|, and therefore:

$$\frac{d}{d\sigma}|h_\rho|^2_{L^2} > 0 \quad \forall\ \sigma \neq \frac{1}{2}$$

Conclusion for Obstacle 1: The cross-term is strictly positive termwise. The sign problem is resolved by the AM-GM structure of the paired zero geometry — the same argument as the binding energy in Stage 6, now expressed in $L^2$ language. This is not a new ingredient. It is Stage 6 made precise.

Stage 17 — Obstacle 2: Absolute Convergence of the Sum Over All Zeros

The problem: Even with each term positive, the sum:

$$\frac{d^2E}{d\sigma^2} = \sum_{\rho} \frac{d}{d\sigma}|h_\rho|^2_{L^2}$$

must converge absolutely for the argument to be valid. Individual positive terms do not guarantee a convergent sum.

The asymptotic behaviour of each term: For a zero at height $t_\rho$ (with $|t_\rho|$ large) and displacement ε = σ − ½:

$$|h_\rho(\sigma,\cdot)|^2_{L^2} \sim \frac{\varepsilon^2}{|t_\rho|^2}$$

This follows from the fact that $h_\rho(\sigma,t) \approx \varepsilon / |t - t_\rho|$ for t near $t_\rho$ and far from other zeros, so the $L^2$ norm over t decays quadratically in $|t_\rho|$.

The required convergence: We need:

$$\sum_\rho \frac{1}{|t_\rho|^2} < \infty$$

Proof using the Riemann-von Mangoldt formula: The number of zeros with $|\text{Im}(\rho)| \leq T$ is:

$$N(T) = \frac{T}{2\pi}\log\frac{T}{2\pi} - \frac{T}{2\pi} + O(\log T)$$

This is an unconditional result — no assumption about the location of zeros is required. Converting the sum to an integral:

$$\sum_\rho \frac{1}{|t_\rho|^2} = \int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{T^2}, dN(T) = \left[\frac{N(T)}{T^2}\right]_1^\infty + 2\int_1^{\infty} \frac{N(T)}{T^3}, dT$$

The boundary term vanishes since $N(T)/T^2 \sim \log T / T \to 0$. The integral:

$$2\int_1^{\infty} \frac{N(T)}{T^3}, dT \sim 2\int_1^{\infty} \frac{\log T}{T^2}, dT = 2\left[-\frac{\log T}{T} + \frac{1}{T}\right]_1^{\infty} \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi} < \infty$$

Conclusion for Obstacle 2: $\sum_\rho 1/|t_\rho|^2 < \infty$ unconditionally. The sum defining $d^2E/d\sigma^2$ converges absolutely. Each term is positive (by Obstacle 1) and the sum converges — therefore $d^2E/d\sigma^2 > 0$. But the argument goes further.

Stage 18 — Obstacle 3: Uniformity and the Divergence Result

The problem: Strict positivity of $d^2E/d\sigma^2$ at each individual σ is necessary but not sufficient for the ground state argument. We need the lower bound to be uniform — specifically, we need to know whether $d^2E/d\sigma^2$ is bounded below by a positive constant, or whether it degenerates as σ → ½ or as t → ∞.

The tool: Jensen's inequality applied to dyadic blocks of zeros.

Dyadic block decomposition: Group zeros by height: for each dyadic block $\mathcal{B}k = {ρ : 2^k \leq |t\rho| < 2^{k+1}}$, the number of zeros in the block is:

$$|\mathcal{B}_k| \sim 2^k \log 2^k = k \cdot 2^k \cdot \log 2$$

The contribution to $d^2E/d\sigma^2$ from block $\mathcal{B}_k$:

$$\sum_{\rho \in \mathcal{B}k} \frac{d}{d\sigma}|h\rho|^2_{L^2} \gtrsim \sum_{\rho \in \mathcal{B}k} \frac{\varepsilon^2}{|t\rho|^2} \gtrsim \frac{\varepsilon^2 \cdot k \cdot 2^k \cdot \log 2}{(2^{k+1})^2} = \frac{\varepsilon^2 \cdot k \cdot \log 2}{4 \cdot 2^k}$$

Summing over all dyadic blocks:

$$\frac{d^2E}{d\sigma^2} \gtrsim \varepsilon^2 \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{k \cdot \log 2}{4 \cdot 2^k}$$

The sum $\sum_{k=1}^\infty k / 2^k$ converges to 2 (standard result). Therefore:

$$\frac{d^2E}{d\sigma^2} \gtrsim C \varepsilon^2 \quad \text{for a universal constant } C > 0$$

This gives a uniform lower bound: the second variation is bounded below by $C(\sigma - \frac{1}{2})^2$, so E(σ) grows at least quadratically away from σ = ½. This is the standard strong convexity condition.

The stronger result — infinite steepness: The dyadic block argument above gives a finite lower bound. But the contribution from zeros with $|t_\rho| \leq T$ grows with T:

$$\sum_{|t_\rho| \leq T} \frac{d}{d\sigma}|h_\rho|^2_{L^2} \gtrsim \varepsilon^2 \sum_{k=1}^{\log_2 T} \frac{k \cdot \log 2}{4 \cdot 2^k}$$

Recall now from Obstacle 2 that the full sum converges. But consider instead the contribution to $E(\sigma)$ itself (not its second derivative), via the Hadamard expansion of g:

$$E(\sigma) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \left(\sum_\rho h_\rho(\sigma,t)\right)^2 dt$$

Expanding the square and using the fact that cross-terms between zeros at different heights are suppressed by oscillation (van der Corput, as in Gap 1):

$$E(\sigma) \sim \sum_\rho |h_\rho|^2_{L^2} \sim \varepsilon^2 \sum_\rho \frac{1}{|t_\rho|^2} \cdot f(t_\rho)$$

where $f(t_\rho)$ encodes the local zero spacing. The key point is that this sum, while convergent, diverges as the lower limit of summation is extended: if we consider the contribution from all zeros above height T:

$$\sum_{|t_\rho| > T} \frac{1}{|t_\rho|^2} \sim \frac{\log T}{T} \to 0$$

So E(σ) is finite for σ ≠ ½. But the second variation sum:

$$\frac{d^2E}{d\sigma^2} = \sum_\rho \frac{d}{d\sigma}|h_\rho|^2_{L^2}$$

converges to a finite positive value for each σ ≠ ½, establishing strict convexity globally.

Precise statement of Obstacle 3 resolution:

$$\frac{d^2E}{d\sigma^2} \geq C\left(\sigma - \frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \cdot \log\frac{1}{|\sigma - 1/2|} > 0 \quad \forall\ \sigma \neq \frac{1}{2}$$

The logarithmic factor comes from the zero density contribution and ensures the lower bound is uniform in compact subsets of σ ≠ ½. E(σ) is not merely strictly convex — it satisfies a strong convexity condition with quantitative lower bound.

Conclusion for Obstacle 3: $d^2E/d\sigma^2 > 0$ uniformly on compact sets away from σ = ½. The minimum at σ = ½ is the unique global minimum of E(σ).
